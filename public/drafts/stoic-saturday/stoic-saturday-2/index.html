Stoic Saturday #2

April 25, 2026 · 2 min · 314 words · Phil Huffman

“It is not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things.” — Epictetus, Enchiridion

You get cut off in traffic. Your boss sends a terse email. The waiter brings the wrong order.

You tell yourself these things make you angry, anxious, frustrated. They don’t. They are just events. Neutral facts in a vast and indifferent universe. What disturbs you is the story you instantly, automatically attach to them.

The driver disrespected you. Your boss must be unhappy with your work. The waiter ruined your evening. None of these narratives are in the event itself. You manufactured them in milliseconds, and your body responded to the fiction as if it were fact.

Epictetus called this the difference between impressions and judgments. An impression is: “That car moved in front of mine.” A judgment is: “That driver is an idiot who thinks he’s better than me.” The first is observable reality. The second is a story — and it’s the story that raises your blood pressure.

The Stoics weren’t asking you to pretend everything is fine. They were asking you to be precise. Separate what happened from what you decided it means. Because once you see that gap, you own it. And if you own it, you can change it.

This isn’t positive thinking. It’s not reciting affirmations while your life burns down. It’s the harder work of noticing how quickly you layer interpretation over reality — and choosing to stop.

Most people live entirely inside their stories. They react to fiction all day long, then wonder why they’re exhausted.

This week’s practice:

For the next seven days, catch yourself in emotional reaction. Pause and write down two things: the bare facts of what happened, and the story you immediately told yourself about it. Don’t try to fix anything yet. Just notice the gap. That’s enough. That’s the work.

See you next Saturday.

— Phil
